## What is bruxism or teeth grinding?

Essentially, bruxism is exactly what it sounds like: clenching or grinding your teeth frequently, often without being fully aware of your actions, especially at night. Our teeth are not designed to withstand this constant force and abrasion.

There are two types of bruxism: sleep bruxism and awake bruxism. Both are associated with the same types and levels of physical damage, but there is a key difference. Sleep bruxism generally stops when the individual wakes up and symptoms gradually improve throughout the day; whereas awake bruxism means clenching the teeth all day long.

Bruxism can be a very serious condition and can cause irreversible damage over time. That is why it is important to recognise the symptoms of bruxism and seek an accurate medical diagnosis as soon as possible, to achieve effective and prompt treatment.

## Some of the most common symptoms of chronic bruxism are:

- Loud teeth grinding and jaw clenching.
- Damaged teeth. This can include chipping, flattening, fracturing, and loosening of the teeth.
- Worn tooth enamel. Enamel is the substance that acts as the outer coating of the tooth. In severe cases, the inner layers of the tooth will be exposed.
- Gum recession.
- Extreme tooth pain or sensitivity.
- Chronic jaw pain and temporomandibular joint disorder. This can lead to symptoms of jaw locking, characterised by the inability to fully open and close the jaw.
- Neck or facial pain. Some people with bruxism may also experience earache, even though the problem does not originate from the ear canal.
- Chronic headaches. For most, these headaches begin at the temples and slowly move inward.
- Cuts and sores from biting the inside of the cheek.

## What is the treatment for bruxism?

Dentists are the ones who check for signs of bruxism during routine dental examinations. If signs of bruxism are detected, our dentists will usually schedule follow-up appointments to determine whether the condition is progressing. Occlusal splints are often the first dental approach used to address bruxism.

In severe cases, dental therapy or medication may be recommended. People with bruxism can choose from a wide range of occlusal splint models to suit their individual preferences.

The best results are achieved with customised splints. These are created using a detailed impression of the patient's mouth, sent to the laboratory and made to the patient's exact measurements.

Generally, the occlusal splint is mainly used at night, during sleep, to prevent the teeth from coming into contact with each other and causing excessive wear. It also helps to relieve the jaw muscles and joints, thus achieving the desired outcome.
